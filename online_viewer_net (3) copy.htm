What Exactly Are PFAS?

A giant chemical family designed to be indestructible ‚Äì and that‚Äôs the problem.

PFAS (per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances) are a family of over 4,600 synthetic chemicals invented in the late 1940s. They include well-known compounds like PFOA, PFOS, PFHxS, and PFNA, used for their resistance to heat, oil, and water (American Cancer Society par. 1) .

Their super-power is an extremely strong carbon‚Äìfluorine bond. That bond makes PFAS great for non-stick pans, waterproof fabrics, and firefighting foam ‚Äî but it also means they almost never break down in the environment or the human body (Bonato et al. par. 2).

PFAS are often described as ‚Äúforever chemicals‚Äù because:

  • the carbon‚Äìfluorine bond is one of the strongest in organic chemistry,
  • they resist heat, sunlight, and chemical attack,
  • they don‚Äôt biodegrade, so they keep cycling through air‚Äìwater‚Äìsoil‚Äìfood.

They are also classified as Persistent Mobile Organic Contaminants , meaning they‚Äôre both long-lived and extremely mobile in water (Bonato et al. par. 1).

How Do PFAS Spread & Reach Us?

From factories to rivers, to crops, to tap water ‚Äî then into our blood.

PFAS contamination is truly global. These chemicals have been detected in oceans, rivers, lakes, groundwater, soil, and wildlife on every continent ‚Äî including remote regions like the Arctic and Antarctic (Bonato et al. par. 2).

Researchers describe three main exposure pathways:

1. Oral

Drinking contaminated water, eating crops grown on polluted soil, fish from polluted rivers, or animal products from exposed herds. Studies identify food and water as the primary exposure routes (Dehghani et al. 6).

2. Inhalation

Breathing air and dust near factories, incinerators, or indoors where PFAS-treated products shed particles into household dust (Dehghani et al. 7).

3. Dermal

Skin contact with PFAS-treated clothing and carpets, cosmetics, and food packaging.

How Do PFAS Affect Human Health?

Cancers, immune disruption, cholesterol changes, and more ‚Äì backed by large studies.

PFAS are not just a theoretical risk. The C8 Health Project ‚Äî one of the largest environmental health studies in U.S. history, with about 69,000 participants ‚Äî evaluated communities exposed to PFOA from DuPont‚Äôs Washington Works plant. The independent science panel found ‚Äúprobable links‚Äù between PFOA exposure and several serious diseases.

These include: Kidney cancer Testicular cancer Ulcerative colitis Thyroid disease High cholesterol Pregnancy-induced hypertension
Cancer classification

In 2023, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) categorized PFOA as Group 1 ‚Äì carcinogenic to humans, based on sufficient evidence for kidney and testicular cancer. PFOS was classified as ‚Äúpossibly carcinogenic‚Äù (par. 4)

Immune system

Elevated PFAS levels are linked to weakened antibody responses and poorer vaccine effectiveness, especially in children, along with possible higher infection risk.

Metabolic & liver

PFAS exposure is associated with higher cholesterol, thyroid problems, and non-alcoholic fatty liver disease due to disrupted lipid metabolism (Dehghani et al. 37).

PFAS, Pregnancy & Child Development

PFAS can cross the placenta and show up in breast milk ‚Äî affecting the next generation.

A key concern in your research is how PFAS reach fetuses and infants. Studies show PFAS can cross the placenta, appear in cord blood, and be detected in breast milk at tens to hundreds of ng/L.

üß¨ What happens during pregnancy?
‚ñº
  • PFAS can interfere with PPAR signaling , which helps regulate lipid metabolism and energy balance.
  • Higher maternal PFAS levels have been linked to lower birth weight in multiple cohorts.
  • Exposure has been associated with pregnancy-induced hypertension and gestational diabetes in some studies.

A Canadian study (MIREC) found that each 10-fold increase in maternal PFOA was linked to a measurable drop in birth-weight z-score. In an Ohio Valley cohort, PFOS exposure was associated with a ~29 g decrease in fetal weight per log-unit increase (Szilagyi et al. 6).

Breast milk

PFOA and PFOS are frequently detected in breast milk. Concentrations and trends over months vary by chemical, but PFAS clearly reach nursing infants (Szilagyi et al. 6).

Hormones

PFAS mixtures have been linked to altered sex hormone levels in teens and changes in markers along the hypothalamic‚Äìpituitary‚Äìgonadal axis (Dehghani et al. 28).

Takeaway PFAS exposure isn‚Äôt just about today‚Äôs adults. It can shape fetal growth, birth outcomes, hormone balances, and long-term risks like obesity and cardiometabolic disease in children (Szilagyi et al. 6).

Real-World Case Studies

Mid-Ohio Valley, USA (DuPont Washington Works)
For decades, DuPont‚Äôs Washington Works plant in West Virginia released PFOA (C8) into the Ohio River and local environment. Between 1951 and 2003, an estimated 1.7 million pounds were emitted. Residents in six nearby counties had average blood PFOA levels of about 32.9 ng/mL ‚Äî compared with 5.2 ng/mL for the general U.S. population (Bonato et al. 5).

Community lawsuits led to the C8 Health Project, which collected blood samples and health data from around 69,000 people, ultimately confirming probable links between PFOA and multiple diseases (Frisbee et al. 1875-1877).

Deep dive

Internal company documents uncovered in lawsuits showed DuPont and 3M had evidence of birth defects, liver damage, and high worker blood levels by the 1960s‚Äì1980s but continued marketing PFAS as safe and under-reported risks to regulators (Kluger par. 6).

Can We Remove PFAS From Water?

Chaudhary et al. say yes ‚Äì but it‚Äôs expensive, and someone has to pay.

Conventional wastewater treatment can‚Äôt eliminate PFAS. They pass through standard processes essentially unchanged. But advanced technologies can strip PFAS from drinking water.

Dual-functional nanofiltration membrane
A 2023 study developed a mixed-matrix membrane using carbon nanotubes and ceramic components. In lab tests, it removed about 90‚Äì99% of PFOA while maintaining high water flux and good stability ‚Äî a promising tool for advanced treatment plants.

The fact that we need high-tech nano-engineered membranes at all shows how hard it is to clean up ‚Äúforever chemicals‚Äù once they‚Äôre loose in the environment.

What Can Ordinary People Do?

Individual choices can lower exposure, but systemic change is essential.

No one can completely avoid PFAS ‚Äî they‚Äôre too widespread. But you can lower your personal exposure and support policies that tackle the root causes.

At home
  • Use PFAS-free cookware (e.g., stainless steel, cast iron, true ceramic).
  • Limit use of stain-resistant sprays and waterproofing treatments.
  • Reduce fast-food packaging exposure when possible.
Water
  • Check if your utility has published PFAS testing results.
  • Consider a filter certified to reduce PFAS (often reverse osmosis or carbon).
Civic action
  • Support strong PFAS regulations and cleanup funding.
  • Back ‚Äúpolluter pays‚Äù policies so communities aren‚Äôt stuck with the bill.
